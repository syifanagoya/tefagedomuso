Ed Subitzky Overview and Wiki

Early Life and Education

Ed Subitzky, born Edward Jack Subitzky, embarked on his journey in Mount Vernon, New York. His early exposure to comic books and Mad magazine, particularly the works of Harvey Kurtzman, shaped his creative path.

Subitzky’s academic pursuit at Binghamton University, where he majored in mathematics and delved into philosophy, further honed his analytical and creative skills. His education at the School of Visual Arts under the guidance of notable cartoonists like Bob Blechman and Charles Slackman played a pivotal role in his artistic development.

National Lampoon Magazine

Subitzky’s career took a significant turn in the early 1970s when he joined National Lampoon magazine as a long-term contributing editor.

Here, he worked alongside well-regarded humor and comedy creators such as Henry Beard, Doug Kenney, Michael O’Donoghue, P.J. O’Rourke, and Michael Gross. His contributions to the magazine were diverse, including comic strips, cartoons, and written articles.

Subitzky’s work appeared in almost every issue of the magazine during his tenure, and he even guest-edited the Old Age issue in September 1974. His name remained on the masthead of National Lampoon through the 1970s, the 1980s, and almost up to the point of its eventual demise.

Radio and Television

Subitzky also made his mark in radio and television. He was a writer and occasional performer on The National Lampoon Radio Hour, which ran from 1973 to 1974.

He conceptualized and wrote the Public Disservice Announcements, parodies of public service announcements, and other pieces. Subitzky also conceived, wrote, and starred in a radio play titled The Sluts from Space, a spoof of science-fiction/horror dramas.

In television, he worked multiple times with David Letterman, contributing to both The David Letterman Show and Late Night with David Letterman. His unique comedy sketches, including The Imposter series, showcased his creative versatility.

Contributions to Other Media

Subitzky’s talents were not confined to just National Lampoon and television. He had several comic strips published as Op/Art in the op-ed pages of The New York Times during the 1990s.

He also contributed to other magazines, including Cavalier, Scanlan’s Monthly, The Magazine, The New Yorker, Tricycle: The Buddhist Review, and Natural History. His work in these publications further solidified his reputation as a versatile and creative artist.
